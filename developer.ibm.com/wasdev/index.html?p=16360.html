Evolving Java EE applications to microservices: Costs and surprises


Adam_P
Published on December 18, 2015
0 Comments

I was talking with some customers this week about evolving their applications to microservices. Some interesting points came out in our discussions:

  • Microservices aren’t free!
  • Java EE and Liberty provide all you need to ‘do microservices’.

Initially I was surprised by these, aren’t they obvious? Well, no. We’re so used to working in the microservices space that some things are starting to be taken for granted. We forget that not everyone is up to speed with current best practices, in fact a lot of people are just starting their journey with microservices. So with that in mind, I thought I’d just look at the first of these in more detail.

Microservices aren’t free!

I’ve mentioned this before in my “Rules of Engagement” – Principles and Considerations blog post, but it’s worth reading Martin Fowler’s Microservice Premium article. The graphic below (taken from that article) I think neatly summarises things:

Starting from the left-hand side, you have a monolithic application that isn’t too complex. Rolling out updates to it and adding new function can be done in an acceptable timeframe. In fact, if you’re honest, it may be that, actually, life is good enough at this point and you’re just using this as an excuse to ‘do microservices’ :-).

However, as the complexity of your existing application grows, the potential productivity gains that can be made by evolving to a microservices architecture increase. I’ve highlighted the word ‘potential’ because that’s what it is – a possible productivity increase. Microservices involves new technologies and, therefore, requires new skills. Any potential gains can be reduced or even eliminated if you can’t meet these new needs.

Finally, notice how the productivity levels out for both microservices and the monolithic app? I think this nicely illustrates that microservices allow you to achieve greater productivity relative to a complex monolithic application. In both cases, though, you will learn to get the job done, evolve new processes, and achieve a steady level of productivity. Microservices should still give you a boost for more complex applications.

Why the Image service?

Given that microservices cost, a colleague of mine recently asked me why I’d picked the Image service as my first one to evolve from the Plants by WebSphere monolith. The conversation was something like this:

Her : ‘So why did you pick the Image service … ?’
Me : ‘… because it was a good starter – static content, simple business logic, no IP to protect’
Her : ‘Yeah, but it’s so simple that you haven’t reduced the complexity of your application, it’s going to cost more to do the service than you’re gaining.’
Me : ‘Complexity is only one thing, by ultimately moving the Image service to Bluemix I’m going to be saving on storage and processing’
Her : ‘Ah! You didn’t tell me that!!’

Which is a fair point. It is mentioned in my previous articles but it probably also needs calling out in the context of this discussion on the cost of microservices. Productivity is only one measurement of your return on investment for evolving to microservices. Other metrics to consider are:

New APIs = new revenue opportunities?

A microservice can be your entry point to the API economy, when designing your API (and I’ll cover that in another article), ask yourself the following questions:

  • Will I be creating additional business opportunities through my new APIs by being able to offer new services?
  • Will I be able to reduce existing maintenance costs by converging/reusing services?

Making or saving money, either one is good news!

Using Bluemix = resource savings?

By ultimately deploying your service to Bluemix, you might be able to save on resource costs. In the case of the sample Image service used in Plants by WebSphere, it’s not exactly a huge saving. However, imagine that this Image service is for a full product catalog, containing multiple image resolutions and taking gigabytes of storage… Letting someone else take care of that storage, and providing the necessary scaling under load, could free up existing hardware for other work or even be dispensed with completely.

So, microservices aren’t free but productivity isn’t the only measure of success. Evolving to a microservices architecture and pairing that with Bluemix gives you the potential to generate new revenue, or to make some cost savings.
